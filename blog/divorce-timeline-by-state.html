How Long Does a Divorce Take? State-by-State Timeline

Compare waiting periods, fastest and slowest states, and what affects your timeline

One of the first questions people ask when considering divorce is: "How long does a divorce take?" The answer depends almost entirely on your state's laws and whether you and your spouse can agree on the terms. Some states have no mandatory waiting period and can finalize an uncontested divorce in weeks, while others require you to wait 6 months to a year before the court will grant a decree.

This state-by-state divorce timeline guide for 2026 breaks down waiting periods, typical processing times, and the factors that can speed up or slow down your divorce.

Factors That Affect Your Divorce Timeline

Even in states with no waiting period, your divorce could take months — or even years — depending on these factors:

1. Contested vs. Uncontested

This is the single biggest factor. An uncontested divorce (both spouses agree on everything) can often be finalized in the minimum time allowed by law. A contested divorce — where you disagree on property, custody, or support — can drag on for 12 months or more in any state.

2. Children and Custody

Divorces involving child custody disputes take significantly longer. Courts prioritize the child's best interest, often requiring parenting evaluations, guardian ad litem reports, and multiple hearings. Some states also impose longer waiting periods when minor children are involved.

3. Court Backlog

Many courts are still dealing with pandemic-era backlogs. Urban counties in states like California, New York, and Florida can have wait times of several months just to get a hearing date.

4. Property and Asset Complexity

If you have significant assets — real estate, retirement accounts, businesses, investments — dividing them fairly takes time. Complex property cases often require appraisals, forensic accountants, and expert witnesses.

5. Spouse Cooperation

If one spouse refuses to respond to the divorce petition, you may need to pursue a default divorce, which adds weeks or months to the process. If a spouse can't be located, you may need to serve by publication, adding even more time.

Typical Divorce Timeline: Step by Step

Here's what a typical uncontested divorce timeline looks like in most states:

  1. Filing the petition: Day 1. You file paperwork and pay the filing fee.
  2. Serving your spouse: Days 1–14. Your spouse must be officially notified.
  3. Response period: 20–30 days. Your spouse has time to respond.
  4. Mandatory waiting period: 0–365 days, depending on state.
  5. Settlement agreement: If uncontested, you submit your agreed terms to the court.
  6. Final hearing: Some states require a brief court appearance; others don't.
  7. Divorce decree issued: Your divorce is final.

How to Speed Up Your Divorce

  • Agree on everything before filing. The more you settle upfront, the faster the process.
  • File in a state with no waiting period (if you meet residency requirements).
  • Use mediation to resolve disagreements faster than court litigation.
  • File all paperwork correctly the first time. Errors cause delays and rejections.
  • Respond promptly to all court requests and deadlines.
  • Consider a joint petition — some states allow both spouses to file together, skipping the service step entirely.

Bottom Line: How Long Will Your Divorce Take?

The honest answer: it depends. A simple, uncontested divorce with no children and no property disputes can be done in as little as 2–6 weeks in states like Nevada or Alaska. A contested divorce with custody battles in a state like California or North Carolina can easily take 1–2 years.
