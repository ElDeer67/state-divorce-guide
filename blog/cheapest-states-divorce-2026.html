Cheapest States to Get Divorced in 2026

Compare filing fees state by state and learn how to save money on your divorce

The cost of getting divorced varies wildly depending on where you live. Divorce filing fees in 2026 range from as low as $70 in some states to over $400 in others — and that's before you factor in attorney fees, mediation, and court costs. If you're looking for the cheapest states to get divorced, this guide ranks states by filing fees and shares practical tips to keep your total costs as low as possible.

Filing Fees vs. Total Divorce Costs

It's important to understand the difference between the filing fee and the total cost of your divorce:

  • Filing fee: The court charge to submit your divorce petition. This is a fixed, one-time cost.
  • Service fees: $50–$150 to have your spouse officially served with papers.
  • Attorney fees: $3,000–$15,000+ per spouse, depending on complexity.
  • Mediation: $3,000–$8,000 total (split between spouses).
  • Court costs: Additional fees for motions, copies, and hearings.

The cheapest divorce is always an uncontested, no-attorney filing. If you and your spouse agree on property division, custody, and support, you can often complete the entire process for just the filing fee plus minimal court costs.

7 Tips to Save Money on Your Divorce

1. File Uncontested If Possible

An uncontested divorce — where both parties agree on all terms — is by far the cheapest route. Many states offer simplified forms and procedures for uncontested cases, and some don't even require a court hearing.

2. Use Online Divorce Services

Services like CompleteCase, 3StepDivorce, and MyDivorcePapers prepare your forms for $150–$300. Much cheaper than an attorney for straightforward cases.

3. Apply for a Fee Waiver

If your income is below a certain threshold, most states offer a fee waiver (sometimes called "in forma pauperis") that eliminates the filing fee entirely. Ask your court clerk about income eligibility.

4. Choose Mediation Over Litigation

If you can't agree on everything, try mediation before hiring dueling attorneys. A mediator typically costs $3,000–$8,000 total — compared to $15,000+ per spouse for a contested court battle.

5. Do Your Own Paperwork

Many courthouse websites offer free downloadable forms. With a little patience, you can fill out and file the paperwork yourself — no attorney needed for a simple, uncontested case.

6. Agree on Property Division Before Filing

The more you and your spouse can agree on before involving the court, the less time (and money) you'll spend in the process. Sit down and negotiate the big items — house, cars, retirement accounts, debts — before you file.

7. Consider a Legal Document Preparer

In many states, non-attorney document preparers can help you fill out divorce forms for $200–$500. They can't give legal advice, but for straightforward divorces, you may not need it.

Can You Get Divorced in a Different State?

Technically, you can file for divorce in any state where you meet the residency requirement. Some states require as little as 6 weeks of residency (like Nevada), while others require 6 months to a year.

⚠️ Important: Moving to another state just to get a cheaper or faster divorce isn't always practical. You'll need to actually establish residency, which means physically living there for the required period. Factor in the cost of relocating before assuming an out-of-state filing will save money.

Bottom Line: How to Get the Cheapest Divorce

The cheapest divorce in 2026 comes down to three factors: where you file, whether you agree with your spouse, and whether you hire an attorney.

  • Cheapest possible: Uncontested, self-filed in a low-fee state like Wyoming ($70) or Mississippi ($75).
  • Affordable middle ground: Online divorce service ($150–$300) + filing fee in your state.
  • Worth the investment: Mediation ($3,000–$8,000) when you can't agree but want to avoid court.
